Origins



Being a part of Berkeley’s highly politically engaged campus, my team and I noticed people all around us looking for ways to take action and support political causes that they believe in. This generation has strong political opinions, and its members are not afraid to vote, protest and boycott to make their opinions heard. However, the reality is that daily activism is difficult and requires a continuous, sustained effort that the many people just aren't able to commit.

DollarVote wants to combat this friction and allow people to seamlessly integrate political impact into their everyday routine.



We do this through our Chrome extension, which integrates itself naturally into online shopping experiences and informs people about how different brands impact political causes they care about, allowing them to put their dollar towards causes they believe in while staying informed about the impact of their purchases.

THE IDEA



Dollar Vote shows the user relevant data about the political impact of companies as they're shopping online. We filter companies based on the user's personal value profile.

Currently, boycotting a company requires users to:

1. perform research about companies related to causes they care about and keep track of that list.
2. dig into the weighty public donation databases, which are dense and difficult to parse.

This means changing your entire shopping habit, and investing time and effort into researching companies before every purchase.

PRODUCT IN ACTION


Dollar Vote gives the user this information up front in the form of a popup that appears right over the screen they're shopping on. This means that users don't even need to open a new tab to get the information they need to make a purchasing decision they can feel good about.

Easy to scan signals:



Just like the star rating, title, and product image all contribute to a shopper's decision to learn more about and eventually purchase a certain product, our metric acts as an additional piece of data for users to take into account while they're deliberating.

VALIDATION AND PROTOTYPING


While brainstorming different flows and forms our product might take, we had a few core considerations:

1. seamlessly integrate into current shopping experiences for easier adoption

2. provide relevant information just in time and on the spot to maximize the impact on their decision we can have.

3. provide users with action outlets such as alternative products, so they can easily make smarter decisions.

USER RESEARCH

Context


Maya is probably browsing online in her spare time. She is probably procrastinating or taking a break from work, opened a new tab, and decided to search for some stuff on Amazon or her favorite ecommerce website. She’s probably pretty distracted, not really willing to think too deeply about what she’s buying on Amazon. She likely is going to pick one the first few top rated items she sees. Alternatively, she may be shopping for something that she routinely buys. She gravitates towards a brand that she has used before without thinking too much about it.

Mental Model


Maya has been trying to be more politically engaged, but she doesn't have the time or energy. She wants to be a more ethical shopper - she understands that several popular brands contribute significantly to causes that are unsustainable/ do not align with her beliefs. However, her busy daily schedule and limited resources prevent her from making and tangible changes to her daily habits.

Pain Points


Maya doesn’t have time in her day to research the products that she is purchasing. She wants to donate money to political causes that she believes in, but she doesn’t feel like she has enough money to make any impact She wants to be more politically engaged in her daily life, but it is difficult to find outlets to do so regularly. She tries her best to stay informed and up to date about current events around her, but she feels like she doesn’t have ways to act upon what she has read in the news.


To better understand how willing people would be to make changes to their shopping habits, I conducted a survey among UC Berkeley students to understand how much they cared about various issues, and also how much they would be willing to alter their shopping habits to support those issues.

For the causes that people cared deeply about, as expected, they were more willing to actively avoid purchasing from that company.
In another survey conducted by Cone Communication CSR, it was found that:

76% of Americans said that they wouldn't buy a product from a company that they knew didn't support their beliefs.

87% of Americans said they would purchase an item if they found out that a company supports a cause that aligns with their views.



The amount that people said they cared about a certain issue directly correlated with their willingness to boycott it. This shaped the way that we decided to rate the companies, ensuring that the product scores that we show to the user align with their true values. This greatly influenced our onboarding flow, which was initial a "checkbox" system where users could simple click yes/no on a value - now, we ask user to weight the issues on a 1-10 scale, so we capture more of the nuances behind a user's political beliefs.

Onboarding


Being able to accurately gauge the political views and opinions of users is a key part of Dollar Vote. Ivana Ng, product designer at public benefit corporation NavaPBC, and a mentor for our team, gave her feedback from years of crafting onboarding experiences for governmental services that required a lot of information from the user: narrowing the scope, whenever possible, and keeping users from having to fill out any information that they don't feel is immediately relevant to them will help eliminate frustration and decrease drop-off during the process.

After brainstorming and testing different ideas for gathering sufficient information without overwhelming the user, we implemented a 2-step onboarding process which allows users to first select which topics they're interested in delving deeper into, and then answer the tagged value-based questions to complete their profile.

CHROME EXTENSION

The information that we present on the popup is to empower users with information, and the ultimate goal is to influence the user's decisions by providing data that is on-the-spot and relevant, while not being invasive and disruptive to their shopping experience.

While designing our application, my team and I were extremely cognizant about the importance of transparency and ethical conduct with both the data that we receive from users, and the data that we're showing in the hopes of influencing shopping decisions. On the "learn more" links, users will be able to read more about our data sources, select information relevant to the company they're exploring, and decide for themselve whether to purchase from them.
