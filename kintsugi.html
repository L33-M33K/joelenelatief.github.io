context


Kintsugi is a smart voice journaling application that uses AI to measure and predict well-being. Our research-backed plans, based off of Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) help users reduce stress and build self-care habits into their daily routine.

Objective: Increase new user retention rates.


At the start, our data showed that a large number of new users (40%) would make a just one or two journal entries before dropping off. My goal was to first understand why these users stopped using Kintsugi, pinpoint user needs, and design & implement a workflow to set users up for success on their self-care journeys with Kintsugi.

Kintsugi is $20 on the app store, so users that have made the investment are interested in the benefits that Kintsugi has to offer. So what was stopping people from building the self-care habits that they set out to when they downloaded Kintsugi, or what expectations were Kintsugi not meeting? Just like getting into a new exercise routine, results aren't immediately obvious; it takes consistency to see growth. How can we set users up to expect and experience growth in the long term?

PROBLEM STATEMENT


How might we best support, equip, & encourage users starting their self-care journey with Kintsugi?



USER RESEARCH



I conducted 30+ user interviews to better understand user needs & frustrations with the existing onboarding & workflow. I also ran campaigns to reach out to engaged users to understand what is clicking for them, and how we can improve.

PROBLEM DEFINITION



I worked closely with clinical psychologist and Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) expert, Mario Lopes, to dig into what successful CBT workflows looks like for different individuals, and how we could adapt the core ideas for Kintsugi.

I worked in an Agile environment to design and implement the new user workflow that we had prototyped and tested. Understanding the context in which I was designing for helped me design for implementation more efficiently.


Phase 1: User Research


To better understand how we could shape user's self-care habits, I first set out to understand their current habits and what resources they used to achieve them.

Here are some of the main ones mentioned:

Pen & paper journals


Purpose: people used their journals as the place for everything from daily thoughts, to goals and future plans, to daily to-do lists.

Pros: easily accessible and no learning curve

Cons: lack of organization (things get lost), difficult to make actionable improvements because "progress" isn't easily defined, often neglected when life gets busy.

Organization apps & planners


Purpose: goal-setting, scheduling, short and long term planning.

Pros: clear functionality and purpose.

Cons: Difficult to see how daily progress ties into larger goals. Users often associated "goals" with more tangible or professional achievements. Just like physical-health goals (eg, "run a 10k", "lose 5lbs"), mental health needs a way to be tracked in order for effective goals to be set and achieved.

Therapy sessions


Purpose: safe space to express emotions and receive advice, accountability for goals.

Pros: professional advice and support

Cons: Not always accessible - therapists, especially, are often prohibitively expensive and scarce in many areas. Therapy has stigmas surrounding it, and people aren't always willing to seek it. Therapy is not a substitute for daily self-care practices.



For people that successfully utilized these resources, it took time for people to learn what is most effective for them and what gives them the most value. Similarly, growing Kintsugi into a natural part of the user's self-care routine also depends on the user discovering what works for them. Further, Kintsugi has an added layer of being an all in one journal that responds, then offers the tools to turn those insights into action.

How many sessions does it take for users to find their flow with Kintsugi?



Reaping the benefits of journaling requires habit and consistency. Data gathered through NIH research, and user interviews I conducted with Kintsugi's engaged users, showed that it takes about 4 days of consistent journaling for a habit to begin to form.

This knowledge helped further define the problem we were solving: guiding new users to the point where they discover their first insight, develop an understanding of how Kintsugi can work for them personally, and begin forming a habit.

"The first entries I made, it took a bit of external motivation from a friend who uses Kintsugi for me to keep coming back. By the 4th session, I was doing it just for me."

"I now use Kintsugi as a tool to work through complicated emotions, but it took several sessions for me to really get used to talking to the app."

"I feel like the app should guide me through my first sessions more. I've used the voice journaling feature for a few weeks now, but I don't really use "goals" or "memories" at all."

KEY INSIGHTS & ACTIONS


1


The barrier to consistent voice journaling was not solely due to the novelty of the voice journaling aspect of Kintsugi, as we initially hypothesized. People that have maintained paper journals were much more willing to openly express their thoughts and emotions.

Action: provide users with more structured prompts and guidance for their first journal entries, to build confidence in expressing themselves.

Users also mentioned things like "I don't have that kind of relationship with Kintsugi yet", when we analyzed their journal entries and provided them with suggestions, suggesting that we needed to work on building trust and establishing a personal relationship with the user. Looking at data, I created a rough "relationship roadmap" to help the team understand what our goals are in each phase of the user journey.


Newbie user (0-2 entries):

building trust and establishing Kintsugi as a knowledgeable guide; give Kintsugi a personality that encourages users willing to open up.

Explorer (3-5 entries):

get to know the user so we can provide personalized recommendations and help them learn more about themselves.

Veteran user (8+ entries):

continue strengthening the relationship through deeply personalized predictory recommendations as we grow to better understand the user and their individual needs

2


The Inspiration feature was our most instantly "sticky" feature. People had an overwhelmingly positive response to the messages shared from our global Kintsugi community. These Inspiration messages also serve as examples to new users on how to utilize the platform.

Action: leverage the community feature to engage new users, and encourage current users to continue sharing their journal entries to increase engagement among the existing user-base.

3


Users expect us to hold them accountable to their goals. Duolingo, an app that helps users learn a new language by making language learning a habit, builds consistency through daily reminders and learning plans - users expect us to offer a similar structure in Kintsugi's workflow.

We needed to establish a relationship with the users. In order to do that, like Duo the friendly owl, Kintsugi needed a personality its own. One that inspires trust and support in the user.

Kintsugi is, like a friend or therapist you would open up to, supportive, caring, knowledgeable - our messaging and visuals needed to support that.

Action: introduce structure and accountability into the workflow, so users build journaling habits, and encourage users along the way.



What makes a successful therapy journey


To understand how we could adapt the research-backed therapy practices for Kintsugi, I worked closely with Mario Lopes, an MD therapist, who explained to me that therapy is, at its core, about getting to know yourself better. The role of the therapist is to facilitate that process. Just like any relationship, Kintsugi should approach getting to know the user in levels. We brainstormed ideas for a "questionnaire" additional feature that would help give us more data so we could give further personalized recommendations.

Feature Feedback:
The questions, which are largely based on building a therapy-patient relationship, were too heavy-weight for new users - we needed to show value before asking for this info.

From this first idea, we began brainstorming ways to get to know the user, but in a less clinical and direct way that tied in our key feature of voice journaling.

PHASE 2: DESIGN AND IMPLEMENTATION



From the user research, we distilled the key elements for the new user workflow, which included establishing a relationship with the user, introducing mechanisms to help users build consistent habits, and provide more guidance to users getting to know the application.

Before I designed this workflow, Kintsugi took users straight from filling out their basic information to the main recording screen, with no further guidance than this welcome message, which contained all the relevant information for the users to understand how to effectively get started - however, offering all the info up front didn't offer users the opportunity to explore as they listened. Additionally, offering all the instructions right up front resulted in a lengthy message - not ideal for a new user looking to jump right in and try the product out.

The new workflow allows the user to "test-drive" the voice-journaling feature right away, and then receive further guidance as they go. The welcome message is still available for users to listen to whenever they want to, but is no longer their sole source of instruction.

Prototyping & Iteration:



Mid fidelity prototypes to test workflows.


Features:

  • tips for first-time journalers
  • custom popups introducing features such as "Memories" with light guidance and information.
  • popups telling users about the benefit of voice journaling, and encouraging them to be consistent.
  • additional screen in the new user onboarding to get to choose focus areas, so we can tailor journal entry prompts to their personal goals.

User feedback

Users needed to be shown more incremental value as we asked for information. Adding an extra step to the onboarding survey asking them for information about their focus areas before we have shown them any tangible value was prohibitive to people completing the onboarding process, and more people dropping off even before they had made their first recording. Additionally, this flow didn't meet the criteria of helping users build their habits incrementally. For the next interation of the workflow, I focused more heavily on getting users recording frequently, spreading out the feature education for users to discover as they explore, and keeping the process lightweight, ensuring that we provide value every step of the way.

#1: STRUCTURE & GUIDANCE



Building and enforcing journaling habits with carefully curated sessions for the user to schedule.

The first 3 sessions that we immediately present the user after their first journal entry (screen 3), are carefully curated and tested list of questions designed to get the user talking. The scheduled sessions encourage accountability; similar to setting up your next few sessions with your therapist, Kintsugi is here to listen and hold yourself accountable to making time for self-care.




#2: INCREMENTAL FEATURE EDUCATION


Success Screens to set users up to on the CBT workflow.

New users who...

  • have just recorded their first journal entry
  • are in "exploratory mode", checking out what Kintsugi has to offer but not ready to take tangible actions
  • have not given us enough entries and data for us to provide them with meaningful insights and recommendations on the "memories" portion

...need further education about how to effectively use the growth features to better be able to experience the long-term value. Success screens offers the user relevant information about each feature while they're exploring, and tell the user that we'll give them better, more accurate results the more they journal.



#3: TYING TOGETHER THE WORKFLOW



Custom notifications to offer personalized recommendations, encourage engagement, and build Kintsugi's personality.

I designed these custom notifications that house recommendations based off of the user's journal entry analyis. I conducted A/B testing with users on usability hub to understand what messaging resonated with users, and I also integrated illustrations, done by artist Ryo Takemasa, to incorporate more a personal touch to the standard iOS push notification that they were in before.

#4 LANDING ON INSPIRATION



Welcoming users to the community.

The new user workflow concludes on the inspiration page, welcoming users to our community. Through user interviews conducted as well as Firebase data, we saw that inspiration is the most visited page. Users were highly engaged in sending their "thanks" and positive messages to other Kintsugi members all over the world.

Additionally, seeing how other people are engaging with Kintsugi is another form of education for new users just getting into journaling. As such, concluding the onboarding flow on the inspiration page serves as a mechanism to engage users in the community early on, and keep them coming back for more.
